About

Currently, I am an undergraduate mechanical engineering student at Columbia University's School of Engineering and Applied Science (SEAS). Before studying at Columbia, I majored in physics at the College of the Holy Cross and was a part of the 3-2 engineering program. The 3-2 engineering program at Holy Cross allowed me to complete my physics major in 3 years, and then transfer to Columbia to study engineering for 2 years. This program allows me to combine a liberal arts education from Holy Cross with rigorous technical learning from Columbia.


Holy Cross—Liberal Arts

Holy Cross is a four-year Jesuit liberal arts college. As a first-year student at Holy Cross, I played the alto saxophone for the Goodtime Marching Band. In the spring, I shifted to performing for the Crusader Pep Band. In my sophomore year, I tutored at the Nativity School of Worcester in math, science, and other subjects as part of the Student Programs for Urban Development. Tutoring at Nativity was a memorable service experience, where I valued motivating and encouraging others to try their best.


I performed research at Holy Cross; my first project was to engineer a datalogging device that would synchronize data collected by a telescope array. This device used an Arduino microcontroller to replace an old Basic STAMP microcontroller for improved speed and reliability. See my portfolio for more details on this technology. Later, I worked on simulating the viewing solid angle of the telescopes in the array. Finally, I modeled the diurnal variance in detected muon flux and determined whether the telescopes performed inconsistently. I modeled the diurnal variance using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT). I created data processing software using Python and then improved it so that modeling occurred automatically after data processing. This research was a formative experience operating primitive technology with real-time data analysis and hardware adjustment.


Columbia Engineering

This fall, I anticipate joining the Columbia Space Initiative (CSI) and Engineers Without Borders (EWB). I believe initiatives such as CSI characterize the progress that engineers dream of achieving when helping communities; I believe in the prospect of service through engineering. As problem solvers, engineers have the tools necessary to make an impact on communities that need help. Our goal is to work together to innovate lasting solutions that make our communities better places for everyone. I look forward to having the opportunity to serve communities from other nations who need help through EWB.
